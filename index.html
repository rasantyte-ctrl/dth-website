As mentioned in the previous chapter, more and more information can be found online as people not only are, but live in online spaces. One of the biggest threats that I can notice to the field is difficulties to obtain trustworthy information. Misinformation, disinformation and mal-information are not new concepts, but I believe that new technologies (ex. deep-fakes) can make it progressively more difficult to find reliable information as technology improves. The main difference between the previously mentioned terms is that misinformation is something false that a person spreads while believing in it; meanwhile disinformation is any information that someone spreads while being absolutely aware that it is not true (Media Defence). Assuming that collecting various data for research is going to stay online, I see possible risks for both of those types of false information spreading. Controlling and gatekeeping information can be another huge threat: if we expect for Artificial Intellect capabilities to progress, countries like Russia, already described as â€œthe cyber gulagâ€ (Euronews), or China (RSF) and their leaders can install even stricter restrictions and surveillance online. Essential to learn: technologies in intercultural studies

The only cure from such gatekeeping of information and access to people is using critical, computational thinking, and embracing the real world. We, researchers, have to ask ourselves: is there any possible intent for the informants to hide or change facts? How to create trustworthy relationships with informants online? How to trust textual information, photos and videos as new technologiesâ€™ quality to generate them with AI improves? Asking these questions becomes an important task for everyone who works or plans to work / study in the intercultural field. Jeannette M. Wing states that computational thinking can help us â€œreformulat[e] a seemingly difficult problem into one we know how to solveâ€œ (33). Since people are able to â€œbuild systems with functionality limited only by our imaginationsâ€œ (35), it suggests possible tampering with certain functionalities - so it is crucial to keep questioning how each new technology, created by humans, works, what it possibly gatekeepers, and how human biases get integrated into them. Yet another problem, stemming out of this, is that people can then use these imperfect & possibly-tampered-with technologies.

Can the field be immune to change?

Due to the inability to generalize research findings in different contexts, interculturalists will always be necessary to study different people cooperating in a certain time and place. As long as there is no technology, which could evaluate every single personâ€™s relation to another individual, an interculturalist will be necessary like, in my terms, a social psychologist who tries to unravel the social situation, put it in understandable-for-humans words, and solve intercultural problems or notice interculturally available opportunities. Because of that, my opinion stands firmly that, unless we reach a dystopian reality with technology dominating over all of our privacy, including thoughts and biases, interculturality will be a field that is more-or-less immune to changes in the technological sector. That is, of course, if researchers and interculturalists learn to skim through (or rather swim through) the ever-filling sea of mis- and disinformation until personal truths are found, possibly while using the computational thinking method, and not forgetting to face people in the real world.


Conclusion
To summarize, intercultural studies focus on relations of difference between us and them, taking into account both our and their histories and experienced changes, and even though humanities used to follow a more rigid mindset of putting different groups of people in very particular boxes (ex. Hofstedean approach), in the contemporary world a lot of attention is paid for in-depth understanding of particular situations and avoiding generalizations as much as possible. The field that I am currently studying is, in my opinion, very academical, because we are trying to critically reflect and study otherness, but it has huge practical value - to understand those that seem different from oneself - in the corporate world or any big- or small-scale social reality that a person needs to face - from individual interactions of people from different cultural groups to national countries to transnational organizations.
